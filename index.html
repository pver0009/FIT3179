Australian Cost of Living & Purchasing Power Analysis

Understanding regional cost variations and affordability across Australia

This interactive dashboard visualizes cost of living indices, income levels, and expenditure patterns across Australian states and territories. Designed for Australians seeking to understand regional affordability, it enables informed decisions about living locations and financial planning through comparative visualizations of household spending and earnings data.

Weekly Earnings by State and Gender

This chart shows weekly earnings across Australian states and territories, with filters for gender and state selection. ACT and Western Australia show the highest earnings, while Tasmania has the lowest. Gender pay gaps are visible across most states.

How the Average Weekly Wage is Allocated

Based on the average Australian weekly wage of $2,010, housing costs consume the largest share at $452.25 per week (22.5%), reflecting the significant pressure of housing affordability on household budgets. Essential living costs including food (16.6%), transport (14.5%), and housing collectively account for over half (53.6%) of weekly expenditure, leaving limited discretionary income. Recreation represents the fourth largest expense at 12.1%, indicating Australians prioritize leisure activities despite cost-of-living pressures. The remaining 34.3% is distributed across necessities like healthcare, education, and household operations, with tobacco products constituting the smallest allocation at just 0.9% of spending.

Regional Cost of Living Comparison

This map shows the relative cost of living across Australian capital cities, with Sydney and Canberra showing the highest CPI indices. The size of circles represents population weight, highlighting where most Australians experience cost pressures.

Inflation Trends Over Time

Australia experienced significant inflation peaks in 2022-2023, with both headline CPI and trimmed mean measures showing sustained pressure. Recent data indicates a gradual return towards the target inflation band.

Weekly Earnings vs Essential Spending by State

Western Australia and ACT show the highest weekly earnings, while Tasmania has the lowest. However, higher earning states also face proportionally higher housing and essential living costs, affecting overall affordability.

Living Cost Indexes by Household Type

Employee households have experienced the highest living cost increases, particularly during 2022-2023. Pensioner households show more moderate increases, reflecting different spending patterns and index calculation methods.

Household Spending Composition Over Time

The data reveals a fundamental restructuring of Australian household budgets over four decades, with housing costs nearly doubling from 12.8% to 22.5% of total expenditure. This dramatic increase has been partially offset by significant reductions in food spending (down from 19.7% to 16.6%) and transport costs (down from 16.3% to 14.5%). Clothing and footwear experienced the most dramatic decline, falling from 6.5% to just 3.1% of household budgets, reflecting both globalization and changing consumption patterns. Meanwhile, essential services like healthcare and education have steadily increased their share, rising from 3.9% to 5.8% and 0.9% to 3.1% respectively. The data clearly illustrates how housing affordability pressures have reshaped spending priorities, forcing households to allocate less to discretionary categories like furnishings and clothing. This structural shift highlights the growing financial burden of housing on Australian families over the past 40 years.

Cost Pressures by Category and Household Type

Insurance, financial services, and alcohol/tobacco create the highest cost pressures across most household types. Pensioners face particularly high pressure from alcohol/tobacco costs due to excise taxes.

Current Inflation by Spending Category

The data reveals significant divergence in inflationary pressures across spending categories, with alcohol and tobacco experiencing the highest annual increase at 5.7%, closely followed by education at 5.5%. These substantial rises in discretionary spending categories contrast sharply with transport costs, which have decreased by 2.6% annually due to fuel price reductions. Health expenses continue to outpace general inflation at 4.1%, reflecting ongoing cost pressures in medical services and insurance. Essential categories like food (3%) and housing (2%) show moderate but persistent increases, contributing substantially to overall inflation given their significant weight in household budgets. The divergence between categories highlights the uneven impact of inflation across different aspects of household expenditure, with some areas providing relief while others create additional financial pressure.

Data Sources: Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) - Consumer Price Index, Average Weekly Earnings, Household Expenditure Survey, Selected Living Cost Indexes
